South African P2P Asset Marketplace
Venture #1 · Validation Phase · Durban-first

The Opportunity

South Africa has millions of underutilized assets — vehicles, tools, equipment, spaces, electronics — sitting idle while people who need them can't afford to buy them. I'm building the infrastructure to connect asset owners (Hosts) with people who need temporary access (Guests), creating income for owners and access for everyone else. Think Airbnb, but for everything.

For Hosts (Asset Owners)

  • List any asset: vehicle, tool, space, equipment
  • Set your own price and availability
  • Earn passive income from idle assets
  • Protected by transaction insurance

For Guests (Renters)

  • Access what you need, when you need it
  • No long-term commitment or ownership cost
  • Verified listings and secure payments
  • Affordable short-term rental rates

Mistakes that kill ambitious people in your position

1. Mistaking motion for progress

Being busy is not the same as executing. Every day, ask: did I move my top 3 priorities forward? If not, the day was wasted regardless of how full it felt.

2. Letting academics slip while building

Your degree is your credibility infrastructure. Businesses can pivot. A failed module costs you time, money, and momentum. Academics come first in time allocation — every single week.

3. Scaling the lending business too fast

One bad debt spiral can wipe months of profit and capital. Grow the loan book at 20% per quarter maximum until your credit scoring system is proven. Slow is smooth, smooth is fast.

4. Building a product before validating demand

The marketplace is an idea until 10 real hosts tell you "I would pay for this." Do the validation conversations now. Build nothing until you have proof of genuine demand.

5. Underestimating the logbook deadline

The TVET logbook is urgent and non-negotiable. Missing it has real consequences. Treat it like an exam you haven't started studying for — close it out before anything else this week.

The honest truth

You are doing something genuinely hard: studying full-time, running two businesses, job searching, and completing a logbook — all simultaneously. Most people can barely maintain one of these. You're running all four.

The real risk is spreading too thin and excelling at nothing. The antidote is ruthless prioritization: academics first, logbook second, job search third, businesses fourth — in that order, every single week, until exams are done in June.

After June, rebalance. Until then — protect your degree. It is the lever that makes everything else possible.
